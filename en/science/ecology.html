🌱 Why study ecosystems?

Ecosystems show us how all living things are interconnected. Why would losing bees cause a food crisis? Why does deforestation change the climate? Why do invasive species collapse ecosystems? The answer is always the same: everything is connected. Understanding ecosystems is essential for addressing today's most urgent challenges — climate change, biodiversity loss, and sustainable development.

⚡ 30-second summary
  • Trophic levels: Producers → primary consumers → secondary consumers → decomposers
  • 10% Rule: Only ~10% of energy passes to the next trophic level. Higher = less energy
  • Carbon cycle: CO₂ ↔ photosynthesis/respiration ↔ organic matter ↔ combustion
  • Nitrogen cycle: N₂ → nitrogen fixation → ammonium → nitrates → organisms
  • Biodiversity: Genetic + species + ecosystem diversity. Threats: habitat loss, invasive species, pollution

1. Ecosystem Components

An ecosystem is all the living organisms (biotic factors) and non-living components (abiotic factors — sunlight, water, soil, temperature) in an area, interacting together.
  • Producers: Make their own food via photosynthesis (plants, algae, cyanobacteria).
  • Consumers: Obtain energy by eating others.
    • Primary consumers: Eat producers (herbivores — deer, rabbits)
    • Secondary consumers: Eat primary consumers (omnivores/carnivores)
    • Tertiary consumers: Top predators
  • Decomposers: Break down dead organic matter, returning nutrients to the soil (bacteria, fungi).

2. Food Chains & Food Webs AP Exam

A food chain shows a single path of energy transfer: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Hawk

A food web shows all overlapping food chains in an ecosystem — a more realistic picture of feeding relationships.

Arrows in a food chain/web point in the direction energy flows (from eaten to eater).

Example food chain: Phytoplankton → Zooplankton → Small fish → Large fish → Shark

3. Energy Flow — The 10% Rule AP Exam

Only about 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. The other 90% is lost as heat (metabolism, movement).

Ecological pyramid: Energy decreases at each higher level → pyramids of energy/biomass/numbers all taper toward the top.

Example: If producers have 10,000 kcal of energy:
Primary consumers receive ~1,000 kcal → Secondary consumers ~100 kcal → Tertiary consumers ~10 kcal.

4. Nutrient Cycles

Unlike energy, matter is recycled through ecosystems.
  • Carbon cycle: Photosynthesis (CO₂ → organic carbon) ↔ Respiration/decomposition (organic carbon → CO₂). Burning fossil fuels adds extra CO₂.
  • Nitrogen cycle: Nitrogen gas (N₂) → fixed by bacteria → absorbed by plants → returned to atmosphere by denitrifying bacteria.
  • Water cycle: Evaporation → condensation (clouds) → precipitation → runoff/groundwater → evaporation again.

5. Biodiversity & Ecosystem Stability

  • Biodiversity: Variety of species in an ecosystem. Higher biodiversity = greater resilience.
  • Keystone species: Species that has a disproportionately large effect on its ecosystem (e.g., wolves in Yellowstone).
  • Threats: Habitat destruction, invasive species, pollution, climate change, overexploitation.

Practice Questions

Q1: In the food chain: Grass → Rabbit → Fox → Eagle, if the grass contains 100,000 kcal, how much energy reaches the eagle?
Answer: Rabbit gets 10,000 kcal, Fox gets 1,000 kcal, Eagle gets 100 kcal (10% rule applied three times).
Q2: Why are there fewer organisms at higher trophic levels?
Answer: Because energy is lost (~90%) at each transfer, there is less energy available to support organisms at higher levels.
💡 Study Tip: Remember that in food chains, arrows represent energy flow, not "who eats whom" (even though they're the same direction). The 10% rule is why top predators are rare — it takes enormous amounts of primary productivity to support even a single apex predator.
Pre-Test Checklist
  • Food chain: producers → primary consumers → secondary consumers → decomposers
  • Energy efficiency: ~10% of energy transfers to the next trophic level
  • Ecosystem balance: population changes trigger feedback mechanisms
  • Matter cycles (carbon, nitrogen); energy does NOT cycle — flows one-way
